General Teaching Points

Documenting a patient's declining your recommendation

Patients frequently do not like our advice. They almost always have the right to not take our advice. A petient who declines admission does not always have to be signed out AMA. It is also important that you remember that an AMA form does not necessarily protect you from liability.

Consider the case of a patient with an infection. The patient has an elevated heart rate, white blood count, and lactate. It is unclear to you whether the patient will respond adequately with oral medications, especially with his comorbidities. You recommend admission but the patient feels better after IV fluids and wants to go home. You give him IV antibiotics and discharge him. You document that the patient was offered admission but chose to be discharged. This is not an unreasonable course of action. Some providers might choose to have the patient sign out AMA. Other providers would see this as "shared decision-making". The important thing is documenting that the patient made an informed decision. An AMA form can help document the discussion but it is not necessary. Although the patient made do fine, it is also possible that the patient will get significantly worse at home and return significantly worse. Although you may think that the patient made an informed decision, the patient may say that he was unaware that he was septic, that he did not realize his comorbidities put him at higher risk, or how severe the consequences might be if he did get sicker at home.

Shared decision-making is a wide spectrum with the AMA form is at one end and a boiler plate statement of "The patient acknowledged the plan and agrees" at the other. However, there are varying degrees of documentation in the middle that depend of the risk involved. You should appreciate the nuances of when more thorough documentation is required without resorting to an AMA form.

Documenting negative findings

Patients often arrive with a concern about a specific diagnosis, whether from a Google search or from a provider who referred them to the ER. Often, this concern is unfounded. It is wise to be careful to document specific negative findings in the history and exam which do not support the diagnosis. Examples include:
Chief complaintPresenting ConcernNegative findings
Chest painHeart attackVery brief pain
Lack of associated symptoms
Lack of exacerbation with exertion
Leg painDVTLack of leg swelling, calf tenderness, discoloration
Negative Homan's
Abdominal painAppendicitisGood appetite, No rebound or guarding

To only note that the patient has positive signs such as chest wall tenderness, positive straight leg raise, or no BM for 4 days risks confirmation bias. Surely, you also looked for evidence of the patient's concern, and finding none then reassured them. You should document these negative findings in your HPI and physical exam, and then, even better, list in the MDM all of the findings that argue against their concern.
