    Core Concepts

    • Atoms form bonds with each other to combine to form molecules.
    • There are two main types of atomic bonds: covalent bonds and ionic bonds.
    • An ion forms when an atom gains or loses an electron.
    • Different atoms form different numbers of bonds.
    • Valence electrons are the electrons in the outer shell of atom.
    • The number of bonds an atom makes is dependent on its valence electrons.
    • Elements are grouped in columns in the periodic table by the number of valence electrons.
    • A noble gas has a full outer electron shell and does not form bonds with other elements.
    • The elements in the first column form 1 bond.
    • Atoms can form single, double or triple bonds.
    • There are several different ways to draw the structure of a molecule.

    Terms


    A molecule is made of two or more atoms that are chemically bonded together.



    A compound is a substance made of all the same molecule.



    The electrons in an atom's outer shell. The valence electrons are the ones that are chemically active and form bonds between atoms.



    An atom that has gained or lost electrons. If an atom loses an electron it becomes a positively-charged ion. If an atom gains an electron it becomes a negatively-charged ion.



    The bond between two ions. An ionic bond is not as strong as a covalent bond.



    A covalent bond forms when two atoms share electrons. It is stronger than an ionic bond.



    A covalent molecule that has covalent bonds between its atoms. A covalent molecule will not break apart when the molecule dissolves in water.

    Mixtures vs. Compounds


        A compound is made of molecules that are all the same. They have the same atoms in the same structure which are chemically bound together.  A mixture is not chemically bound.

    The Crystal Structure of Sodium Chloride


        Sodium chloride (NaCl) is arranged in such a way that each sodium atom is surrounded by chlorine atoms.  No specific chlorine atom and sodium atom are paired up.  NaCl is not truly a molecule.  NaCl is called the formula unit to clarify that it is not truly a molecule.

    Formation of an Ionic Compound


        The sodium atom (Na) gives up an electron and becomes positively-charged.  The fluorine atom (F) gains an electron and becomes negatively-charged.  The positively- and negatively-charged ions then attract each other.

    Water Molecule


        This is a ball-and-stick model of a water molecule.  The red-colored sphere in the middle represents oxygen and the white-colored spheres represent hydrogen.

    Electron Dot Diagrams


        An electron dot diagram, also known as a Lewis structure, shows only the valence electrons in the outer shell.  Usually there are 8 places in the outer shell for electrons (although only 2 places for hydrogen).  The shared electrons between two atoms are represented by lines indicated a bond between them. Each bond represents 2 electrons.

    Condensed Structures


         Sometimes molecular formulas are written in a way that shows some of the connections within the molecule.  The molecular formula for this molecule is C2O2H4.

    Line Structures for Organic Compounds


        Organic chemistry is the study of carbon-containing compounds.  Because carbon is so common, it is often not written expicitly.  Instead, carbon atoms are represented by the corners when line segments meet.  Because carbon always has 4 bonds, if those bonds are not shown, then a bond with a hydrogen atom is implied.

    Methane Molecule


         There are multiple ways to show the molecular structure.  This picture shows three ways to draw methane.  The image on the left is called a ball and stick model.  Carbon is represented by the larger black sphere and the four hydrogen atoms are represented by the 4 white spheres. The image in the middle shows these attachments in 2 dimensions.  The image on the right is called a space-filling model.  It shows the electron clouds around each atom as they overlap to form bonds.
