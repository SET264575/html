Core Concepts

  • The moon orbits the earth. The earth orbits the sun.
  • The sun is bigger across than the distance between the earth and the moon. There is no way the sun could fit between the earth and moon.
  • During a solar eclipse, the light from the sun is blocked by the moon passing in front of it.
  • During a lunar eclipse, the sunlight is blocked from hitting the moon by the earth passing between the sun and moon.
  • During a total eclipse, the moon completely blocks the sunlight.
  • During a partial eclipse, the moon only blocks part of the sunlight.
  • During an annular eclipse, the moon appears smaller and a ring of sunlight appears when the moon covers the sun.
  • The moon orbits the earth every month but does not cause an eclipse every month because the orbit of the moon is tilted.
  • You should never look at the sun, even during an eclipse because the light can cause permenant eye damage.
  • During a total eclipse, you can look at the sun only for the few minutes while the sunlight is completely blocked by the moon.

The Scientific Significance of Eclipses

Eclipses are significant for at least 5 reasons:
  • Dating historical events
    •       Because eclipses in any one spot are pretty rare, a historical record of an eclipse can help us figure out the dates and times of other events
  • The first evidence that general relativity was correct
    •       Normally, the stars that appear near the sun cannot be seen because the sun is too bright.  Astronomers are able to see the stars that appear near the sun during an eclipse.  Shortly after Albert
      Eisten published his theory of general relativity, astronomers were able to measure the apparent distance between stars near the sun during an eclipse and show that they appeared to have moved.
      The stars didn't really move.  Instead the path the light from the stars followed was bent as the light traveled passed the sun, just as Einstein had predicted.
  • Studying the corona
    •       The corona is the heated gases coming off the sun.  This normally can't be seen except during an eclipse.
  • The mountains on the moon
    •       The last little bit of light that can be seen before totality is light that passes between mountains on the moon.  Before telescopes and space probes were able to map the moon, astronomers used eclipses
      as an opportunity to study the mountains on the moon.
  • Public involvement
    •       Eclipses get people excited about science.

Transit of Venus

      A transit is like a mini-eclipse.  This is a sequence of pictures taken of the planet Venus crossing in front of the sun.  If Venus were much bigger or
      much closer, it would cover more of the sun and we would consider it an eclipse.  Because so very little of the sun is covered by Venus, we call it a transit,
      but it's basically the same thing.  A transit of Venus is very rare.

      Because the planets Mercury and Venus are the only planets that go between Earth and the sun, they are the only ones that transit the sun.  The other
      planets are outside the orbit of Eart and never cross between the Earth and the sun.

Transit of the International Space Station
